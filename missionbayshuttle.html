mission bay shuttle

making an essential public service feel usable, human, and supportive

our redesign of the mission bay shuttle app started with a simple question: how can we make an essential public service actually feel usable, human, and supportive to the people relying on it every day? the existing web app came from a place of genuine care, but it struggled with clarity, wayfinding, accessibility, and real-time trust. we wanted to rethink the entire experience from the perspective of a commuter on the go.

we began with heuristic evaluations, field observations, and ride-along testing — experiencing the shuttle as real users. finding stops was confusing, routes lacked clarity once onboard, and the physical environment wasn't doing much to help. these early insights shaped our north star: make the app feel like a billboard — glanceable, intuitive, and always one step ahead of the rider.

over several weeks, we sketched ideas, created user profiles, built red routes, and prototyped extensively. hallway testing with paper flows helped us refine navigation, and user feedback pushed us toward more personalized features, accessible options, cleaner maps, and clearer live etas.

our final hi-fi prototype introduced a more loyal visual system, a stronger hierarchy, and touch-friendly interactions. the result was an app that met riders where they are — moving, commuting, deciding quickly — and gave them a sense of confidence in a system that can otherwise feel unpredictable.
