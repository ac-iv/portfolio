good graces

your entire church in one app

good graces is a mobile platform i founded to modernize how churches communicate, organize outreach, and stay connected with their members through the week — not just on sundays. it brings messaging, announcements, giving, volunteer coordination, events, and member engagement into one experience designed for everyday use.

the conviction behind it is simple: faith communities deserve technology that feels thoughtful, human, and beautifully designed. most church software is built like enterprise tooling — bloated, dated, and cold. good graces is built like a consumer product people actually want to open.

i lead product, design, and development end to end — from the brand and design system to the react native app — alongside my co-founder on strategy and operations.

the problem

churches are stuck between outdated systems and modern expectations

many churches run on disconnected tools — a website from 2012, a bulk email list, a giving portal that lives somewhere else entirely. communication is fragmented, engagement drops between sundays, and younger generations who expect seamless digital experiences in every other part of life simply drift.

good graces bridges that gap through intentional design and human-centered technology. the goal was never to replace what churches do — it's to help them reach people more effectively, foster real relationships, and strengthen their communities.

design walkthrough

built for churches. designed for people.

every screen in good graces is designed to reduce friction and increase connection. the walkthrough below covers the three core surfaces of the app and the thinking behind each.

01 — community feed

a social experience with a purpose

the feed centralizes updates, prayer requests, volunteer opportunities, announcements, and events into one scrollable home. it borrows the familiarity of social media — cards, avatars, reactions — but strips the noise: no algorithmic chaos, no ads, just the community.

the design challenge was tone. a prayer request and a potluck announcement can't be flattened into identical posts, so the card system uses subtle type, color, and spacing shifts to give each content type its own weight without fragmenting the feed.

02 — home dashboard

everything at a glance, personalized per church

the home dashboard surfaces key tools and updates the moment the app opens — tailored to each church's structure and each member's involvement. a volunteer sees their next shift; a new member sees ways to plug in.

navigation was designed around one rule: nothing important should be more than two taps away. the information architecture went through multiple rounds of card-sorting and prototype testing to get the hierarchy right for both 80-year-old deacons and 16-year-old youth group members — the real accessibility range of a church.

03 — modern giving

generosity without the friction

secure donations, recurring giving, and campaigns are integrated directly into the platform instead of bouncing members to a third-party portal. the giving flow is deliberately calm — clear amounts, transparent confirmation, no dark patterns, no guilt mechanics.

trust is the whole design problem here. every choice — from the typography of dollar amounts to the language of confirmations — was made to feel as considered as a well-designed banking app, because that's the bar people carry in their pocket.

design language

warm, minimal, and quietly confident

the visual identity rejects both the corporate-saas look and the over-stylized "church media" aesthetic. it's built on warmth and restraint: cream backgrounds, a single coral accent, near-black type, and generous space.

palette

a warm off-white foundation keeps screens soft and readable for long sessions. coral is reserved for moments that matter — primary actions, live indicators — so it never loses meaning.

type & voice

a clean grotesque carries the interface; an expressive serif appears sparingly in marketing moments. the writing is plain, warm, and human — no jargon, no church-software speak.

built to last

designed to scale from one congregation to a network

good graces runs on a transparent subscription model that scales with congregation size — accessible to a 100-member church, robust enough for multi-campus networks. the product architecture mirrors that: one design system, flexible per-church configuration, and a react native codebase built to grow.

i — everyday, not just sunday

connection shouldn't spike once a week. every feature is designed for tuesday-afternoon usefulness — the quiet moments where community actually forms.

ii — warmth over efficiency

this is software for relationships, not throughput. when a design decision trades a little efficiency for a lot of humanity, humanity wins.

iii — intimacy at scale

growing from local congregations to national networks can't cost the closeness that makes a church a church. the system scales; the feeling doesn't dilute.
